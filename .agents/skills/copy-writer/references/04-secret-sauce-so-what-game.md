# Add the Secret Sauce: The "So What?" Game

## Connecting Buyer Motivation to Product Value

Now that you understand your buyer's real pains and desires, it's time to connect them to what your product can uniquely deliver. **This is where punchy messaging lives—at the intersection of your buyer's motivation and your product's best qualities.**

## Identifying Your Product's Best Qualities

Best qualities are the unique features and aspects of your product that directly benefit customers. They're the very best things that will entice buyers to learn more—like how a movie trailer highlights the funniest or most action-packed scenes.

### What Best Qualities Are

- The product's secret sauce that differentiates it
- Something your product does better than others
- Things that consistently make customers go "Wow!"

### What Best Qualities Are NOT

- Under-the-hood technical things that don't connect to benefits
- Unsubstantiated claims like "We have the best customer experience"

### Case Study: Digital Workspace for Lawyers

A SaaS company offered a digital workspace for lawyers. When asked what consistently made customers say "Wow!", the team shook their heads: "Oh, it's just the main dashboard where they can see all their work in one place."

The product team wanted to talk about fancy AI-powered features. But the most basic thing—bringing everything together on one screen—was what customers raved about.

**Anything that gets a consistent "Wow!" out of people is part of what makes your product unique.** They focused on this aspect and attracted more customers.

**Key insight:** Tech companies often see the world several years into the future. Customers are often just coming to terms with the basics. Meet your customer where they're at.

## The "So What?" Game

A fast and fun way to translate best qualities into benefits and value propositions.

### How to Play

Imagine sitting across from a bored teenager on their phone. Your goal is to get them to understand why your quality is so cool. Every time you give a reason, they shrug and say "So what?" You dig deeper. They say "So what?" again. Keep going until you can't come up with any more reasons.

### Example: AI Chatbot for Recruiters

**Start:** "It lets you have real-time conversations powered by AI with candidates on the recruitment site."

(So what?)

"So you can automate the manual part of screening candidates."

(So what?)

"So you can speed up your screening process by asking candidates questions while they're on your career site."

(So what?)

"So you can engage talent at the right time."

(So what?)

"So you can hire the best candidates."

(So what?)

"So you can exceed your KPIs for talent acquisition."

(So what?)

"So you can help grow the business with the right people."

### Reading the Results

Your brainstorm will likely be VBF in reverse—starting with the feature, moving into benefits, then expanding into value territory.

1. Write down all your "so whats"
2. **Star the benefits** — superpowers that give new things they can do, be, or feel
3. **Underline the value propositions** — big wins that multiple benefits help achieve

### Pro Tip: Play with Others

Like most things, playing with colleagues yields rich results. Different folks come up with different answers. You'll get more out of this if you include people who don't know much about your product—it keeps you honest and forces you to dig deeper.

## Finding the Right Altitude

Once you have benefits and value propositions, zero in on the strongest ones. Don't try to say everything.

### What Is Altitude?

"Altitude" measures how high-level and broad, or technical and specific, the message is.

| Altitude | Problem |
|----------|---------|
| **Too high in the sky** | Lofty and vague. Focuses on broad value many solutions could claim. "Increase revenue." |
| **Too low in the weeds** | Too close to feature/functionality. "Embed AI-curated data directly into your system." Doesn't show why to care. |
| **Just right** | At the right level for your customer. Not too broad, not too technical. |

### The Goldilocks Rule

- **More technical buyer** → Go lower in altitude (while still showing value)
- **More business-focused buyer** → Go higher in altitude (without straying from what product delivers)

### Example: AI Fraud Detection for Banks

**A+ customer:** Heads of risk at major banks and credit card companies. Not particularly technical. Unaware of AI solutions (new category).

**The "So What?" Game results:**

1. So you can have AI-powered, real-time fraud detection
2. So you can detect fraud faster
3. So you can save time detecting fraud
4. **So you can detect fraud that can't be found in manual document reviews** ⬅️
5. So you can save the headaches of fraud and credit losses
6. So you can reduce risk
7. So you can increase company revenue

**Analysis:**
- #1: Too low in technical weeds (buyer only vaguely knows "AI")
- #6-7: Too high in the sky (too many steps removed from feature)
- #4: Just right (connects to buyer's urgent pain and meets them at their level)

## Balancing Today vs Tomorrow

What if some best qualities haven't launched yet?

### Finding the Golden Thread

Future features don't pop up from nowhere. They're usually a natural evolution of the product and a piece of the overall vision. The essence of the new feature is already in the existing product—it's just being overlooked.

**Ask yourself:**
- What is the benefit of that new feature we're offering in the future?
- How are we delivering it today?
- What are we doing today that has a similar spirit?

### Example: SaaS Collaboration Company

**Future feature:** AI-powered prompts to help people perform tasks better. Live guidance fully integrated into the platform.

**Today:** A library of video resources that customers could use to learn how to do their work better.

**Same benefit:** Helping users be more productive at work. The only difference is how it's delivered.

They included this benefit in messaging to set the stage for the future release. Customers were warmed up to the idea before launch.

### The Key Principle

We often get so focused on HOW we deliver a benefit (the feature) that we miss that we're already delivering it today.

Tee up future releases without overpromising. Start raising awareness of what's possible and what's coming.

## Case Study: ThreatX Differentiates with Value

ThreatX is a leading API and app protection platform. They offered a one-of-a-kind solution but struggled to communicate this over market noise. Every company was trying to explain how their technology was different using the same buzzwords.

**Their unique features:**
- Platform automatically blocked sophisticated attacks in real time
- Managed services at no extra cost

**Customer interviews revealed deeper value:**
- Customers were relieved they didn't have to constantly tinker and hand-hold
- They felt confident their company was truly protected
- Most importantly: **They got their nights and weekends back**

These were the real reasons CISOs were referring ThreatX to other CISOs.

**The messaging approach:**
- Built around customer phrasing: "getting nights and weekends back"
- "Having ThreatX manage the solution for you"
- Focused on customer and value, naturally simplifying and avoiding buzzwords

**Results (2023):**
- Marketing-sourced qualified pipeline opportunities grew ~50% in value
- Volume grew year-over-year
- Despite uncertain economy and flat marketing budget
