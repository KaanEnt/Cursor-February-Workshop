# Feel What Your Buyer Feels: Pains and Desires

## The Core Truth

Right now every potential buyer is thinking, "Eh, who cares?"

Nobody cares about your product unless you give them a damn good reason to. Not because your buyers are selfish. They're just too busy thinking about stuff in their own lives: issues at work, personal plans, problems big and small, things they wish they had.

**There's not a lot of room for your product to muscle its way in there.**

## Tapping Into the Current

Pains and desires are a motivating force inside your buyer's mind, like a strong river current. It's always there, pushing people to make a change.

- **Tap into pains and desires:** Your product benefits from this current and gains traction faster.
- **Just talk about features:** You're swimming upstream. That's a lot of work. You could easily tire out.

## Real Talk Cuts Through the Noise

### The Online Bank Story

A marketer noticed messaging for a new online bank with the headline:

> "Take the fear out of checking your account balance."

It was speaking directly to her soul. She'd never told anyone about her banking shame, but here was this company that knew exactly what she'd been thinking. The messaging kept connecting to her pain: never really knowing what she could afford. It even made a loving joke about English majors and personal finances.

She felt seen and heard. And the more she read, the more she believed the company actually understood her and might have a solution worth exploring.

**If this bank had taken a typical B2B approach:**
> "Drive better financial decision-making" or "Optimize your savings"

Most pains and desires in B2B messaging are sanitized with dry, corporate language that squeezes all the realness out. In an effort to sound serious and smart, it just ends up feeling unrelatable and unemotional.

## The Power of Being Real

### It Attracts the Right People
The online bank's headline wasn't meant for everyone. Accountants and type A people wouldn't connect. But for creative, disorganized people, it was powerfully spot on.

### It Makes People Feel Something
When you read the message, your mind goes back to all the times you felt nervous about personal finances, fanning the flames of desire to finally do something about it.

### It Builds Trust on the Page
Being real shows that you understand their world, which honors their lived experience. Customers want honesty and truth. It's what people expect.

### Case Study: Cybersecurity Company

A cybersecurity company's platform offered a remarkably hands-off solution for busy security teams. During customer interviews, users described the pain the same way:

> "A system they didn't have to hand-hold or babysit"
> "Finally get their nights and weekends back"

This language got incorporated onto their homepage, resulting in an increase in demo requests.

## How to Find Real Pains and Desires

Put yourself in your customer's shoes. Imagine a typical day in their life. See things from their perspective.

### The Questions

**What are their smack-in-the-face challenges?**
Problems that drive your buyer nuts. Daily issues they keep running up against. Workflow breakdowns, pointless manual tasks.

**What wakes them up at 2 a.m. in a cold sweat?**
Big, existential problems that weigh on their mind. Often map to larger business-level challenges. Needing to attract better talent, for example.

**What are their goals on the horizon?**
Larger goals and aspirations. Things they'd like to achieve. Making collaboration easier across departments.

**What are their deep-down desires?**
Personal motivations. How they'd like to change for the better. Being more respected in their job.

**Important:** Look through both a professional AND personal lens. Every "work" problem has a "life" impact too.

## Where to Research

### Interview Current Customers or Ideal Buyers
Speaking to your existing A+ customers is the best way to understand their pains and desires in their own words. Customers describe things in way simpler terms than marketers do. **Some of the best messaging comes directly from customer quotes.**

### Ask Your Sales Team
Salespeople chat with buyers about problems all day. One good conversation can give you insights from both a customer survey AND a messaging test. It's wildly efficient.

### Check Review Sites
G2, Gartner, and other peer review sites show how customers describe challenges they're trying to solve. If you don't have many reviews, check similar products.

### Review RFPs
If you sell enterprise software, RFPs are a treasure trove. Your buyer is literally listing what they're looking for.

## Say It Like You Would at a BBQ

Once you've identified pains and desires, describe them like your customer would.

### The BBQ Test

Read each pain and ask: Would your buyer talk about it this way to a friend at a BBQ on the weekend?

Would they really say "we need to gain operational efficiencies at scale" while drinking a beer? If not, rewrite it.

### Example: Homebuilder Software

**Original pain:** "They need to streamline client communications."

**Reimagined:** Picture the buyer mildly stressed at a BBQ as his phone blows up with calls from clients checking on their home's progress or change orders. These contractors were building people's dream homes, dealing with clients bugging them at all hours.

**BBQ-friendly version:** "I need to get these constant texts, calls, and emails from my clients under control—it's driving me nuts."

### BBQ Translation Examples

| Un-BBQ-Friendly | BBQ-Friendly |
|-----------------|--------------|
| "Boost developer productivity and retention" | "Keep developers happy by letting them focus on building cool sh*t instead of boring manual tasks" |
| "Eliminate the need for multiple tools" | "Take away the frustration of having to log in to multiple tools every day" |
| "Foster continuous engagement among team members" | "Keep everyone on the team involved" |

### Is BBQ Talk Right for Serious Industries?

"Saying it like you would at a BBQ" isn't about being unprofessional or using slang. It's an exercise to get more specific about customer problems by using the right words and acknowledging emotions.

A site reliability engineer's biggest problems?
- The sheer panic of getting a system alert in the middle of the night
- Missing his son's soccer games on the weekends because of work

Every "work" problem has a "life" impact. Every buyer has feelings and a life outside of work that impacts purchasing decisions.

## Highlight Their Most Urgent Pains and Desires

Not all pains and desires are equal.

### The Dentist Analogy

Whenever you get teeth cleaned, the dentist pitches Invisalign for a crooked front tooth. Nice to have, but a lot of money. Then one morning you wake up with an infected wisdom tooth. You'd pay anything to get it removed. Because that pain was urgent.

**Your buyers have:**
- Pains they can tolerate (like a crooked tooth)
- Pains they can't ignore (like a throbbing infected molar)

Focus on the urgent ones that light a fire under their butts to make a change.

### Example: Collaboration Software

Their buyer wanted to create a more inclusive culture. But they had a more urgent pain: productivity was down because everyone was stuck in back-to-back meetings.

People were reminded of it every day as they logged into yet another meeting. The desire for inclusive culture paled in comparison.

### How to Tell if a Pain/Desire Is Urgent

- It's a common reason why prospects reach out to sales
- It gets mentioned a lot in product reviews
- It maps directly to a critical business goal or need
- It reflects a deep personal aspiration or goal

**Make sure the pains and desires are something your product can actually solve.** One desire that came up for collaboration software: "I wish we didn't have to have meetings anymore." Totally legit, but not something the software could deliver.

## It Ain't Always About the Money

A pricing software company helped golf courses use dynamic pricing. Just plugging in their software increased revenue by 6-9% without extra work!

**Assumption:** "Increasing revenue" would be the ultimate goal.

**Reality:** The buyers were busy golf course managers. In customer interviews, they didn't care as much about revenue (they didn't own the course). They were more interested in getting away from their computers and having one less thing to do.

Less work was far more motivating than a 6% revenue increase.

**Customer research is the best way of getting out of our own assumptions.**

## Case Study: Automox Gets Real with Technical Buyers

Automox is a leading IT automation platform. With such a sophisticated solution, it would have been easy to lean into tech talk. Their buyer is very technical and skeptical of big claims.

**But through the process, they discovered deeply human needs:**
- They wanted to stop spending all their time on manual tasks
- They wanted to work on big, strategic projects
- They wanted to play a larger role in their business

So they played into emotion. They touched on what it felt like when buried in tasks or up all night worrying about vulnerabilities. Automox was the only company in its category taking that approach.

**Results:**
- More sales, winning customers' hearts and minds
- Used the messaging for two years (practically a century in tech messaging years)
- 5x the opportunities for the same marketing spend

Justin Talerico, CMO: "This messaging worked so well because buyers felt invited in—Automox was sharing their solution, not selling it."
