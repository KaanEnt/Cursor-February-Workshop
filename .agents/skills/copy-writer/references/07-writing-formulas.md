# Make It Stick: Writing Formulas

## Why Writing Is Hard

Writing can feel challenging because it is challenging, no matter your level of experience. It's something every marketer is expected to do, but most haven't learned how since school (and academic writing isn't even close to the same skill set).

Plus, the more you care about writing, the more likely you are to get stuck in your head.

**This is where formulas swoop in to save the day.** Formulas are like a springboard, allowing you to leapfrog over the heartache of getting started so you can land on a decent working draft faster.

**Approach these formulas like a child with a new toy.** Kids don't worry about playing "the right way." They just pick it up, bang it around, and see what happens—with zero judgment. Do the same with these formulas.

## Writing Your Value Proposition

A value proposition clearly articulates the ultimate value customers can expect. It serves as the opening introduction, so it must be strong enough to stand on its own. It might be the only thing your customer reads.

### Don't Confuse with Taglines

**Taglines convey a vibe:**
- Nike's "Just Do It"
- Zoom's "Meet Happy"

**Value propositions work much harder** to communicate clear, tangible value.

### Three Attributes of Strong Value Propositions

| Attribute | Description | Example (Yes) | Example (No) |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|--------------|
| **Clarity** | People can read it once and understand it. Simple enough for a sixth grader. Not vague or overly clever. | Block sophisticated attacks in real time. | Threat protection. Reimagined. |
| **Uniqueness** | Reflects your unique value. Shows why to choose you over alternatives. | Attract leads with less effort. | Grow your business. |
| **Truth** | No overpromising. Something your product can truly deliver. BS meters are high. | Increase your close rate by 21%. | Get 100% protection against cyber threats. |

### Formula 1: The Command One

Simply describes the big win in straightforward language. Start with a verb, as if commanding customers to achieve something great.

**Prompt:** "Now you can ____."

**Examples:**
- Now you can **build in-demand skills at your own pace.** (online learning platform)
- Now you can **predict and prevent production losses.** (industrial software)
- Now you can **get complex work under control.** (work management software)

**Best for:** All-rounder that works in almost any situation. Great for simplifying headlines for technical products and new technologies.

### Formula 2: The Aspirational One

Taps into larger goals and personal aspirations. We're all trying to become better versions of ourselves. We're not looking to buy software; we're looking for positive change.

**Prompt:** "Imagine if you could ____."

**Examples:**
- **Make every customer interaction count.** (customer experience software)
- **Accomplish more together, faster.** (productivity software)
- **Secure web apps with confidence, not complexity.** (security software)

**Best for:** Adding emotion to headlines. Connecting with readers on a human level. Less-technical buyers.

### Formula 3: The Ultimate One

Focuses on the ultimate end goal. Zooms out to show the big picture of a larger business goal.

**Prompt:** "Ultimately, you will be able to ____."

**Examples:**
- **Create a foundation for sustainable growth.** (sales engagement software)
- **Simplify cloud complexity.** (observability platform)
- **Build a culture of inclusivity.** (employee engagement software)

**Best for:** B2B enterprise software. Value propositions that encompass multiple products.

## Writing Your Benefits

A benefit is like a superpower: something new your customer can do, be, or feel, thanks to your product. Benefits illustrate how your customer can:
- Reduce or solve a meaningful pain
- Achieve a meaningful desire

### Structure

Each benefit should have:
- **Headline:** Use the command formula
- **Supporting message:** 1-3 sentences using formulas below

### Three Characteristics of Strong Benefits

| Characteristic | Description | Good Example | Bad Example |
|---------------|-------------|--------------|-------------|
| **Empowerment** | Elevates customer by leading with what THEY can do, not what the product can do | Get your distributed team on the same page with faster feedback, alignment, and decisions. | Our platform increases productivity by getting your distributed team on the same page. |
| **Directness** | Gets straight to the point. No introductions or stage-setting. | Decrease paper, printing, and handling costs by moving away from hard-copy reviews. | Digitizing processes can help you reduce costs. When you minimize hard-copy reviews, it enables you to decrease paper... |
| **Concreteness** | Stated concretely so you can picture it in your mind. Specific, not abstract. | Remediate thousands of critical vulnerabilities in days, not months. | Transform endpoint protection. |

### Formula 1: The Big Picture

Sets up the benefit as the key to unlocking a larger goal. Great for tying product to meaningful business goals or personal aspirations.

**Prompt:** "[Describe benefit], so you can [larger goal or outcome]."

**Example (Customer service chatbot):**
> Resolve issues quickly and accurately the first time, so your customers become even bigger fans of your brand.

**Best for:** Audiences who aren't very technical and/or unfamiliar with solutions like yours.

### Formula 2: Before and After

Shows how customer's situation will change for the better. "Before" illustrates current pain; "after" shows how it could be solved.

**Prompt:** Brainstorm what life was like before and after your product. Put them next to each other.

**Example (Work management app):**
> Stop bouncing from system to system and feeling bonkers along the way. In your workspace, your team can see everything at once—and handle it with ease.

**Best for:** Helping people grasp benefits of new or emerging technology.

### Formula 3: Benefit Progression

Breaks down a large benefit into three smaller ones. Great for company-level benefits.

**Prompt:** "You can [superpower 1], [superpower 2], and [superpower 3]."

**Example (Data security platform):**
> Locate every threat, figure out what the attackers are after, and keep your data and systems safe for good.

## Choosing the Best Options

### Value Proposition Rubric (Rate 1-5)

| Criteria | Question |
|----------|----------|
| **Clarity** | Can you read it once and understand it? Could a sixth grader understand it? |
| **Uniqueness** | Does it connect to your unique value and stand out? (Check competitive messaging audit) |
| **Truth** | Can we back up this statement with numbers or customer proof? |

**Score 10 or more = on the right track**

### Benefit Rubric (Rate 1-5)

| Criteria | Question |
|----------|----------|
| **Empowerment** | Does it elevate my customer and show what cool new things they can do? |
| **Directness** | Does it get to the main point without dilly-dallying or going off on a tangent? |
| **Concreteness** | Does it have concrete detail, so you can picture it in your mind? |

**Score 10 or more = on the right track**

## Calibrating to Your A+ Customer

"Is this the right approach for sophisticated tech buyers who hate marketing fluff, like developers?"

**The answer:** Most B2B buyers hate marketing fluff. Tech-savvy buyers aren't too smart for benefits—they simply need more advanced benefits.

### The Calibration Rule

- **Less they know about solutions like yours** → Broader message
- **More they know about solutions like yours** → More specific message

### Example: CRM Messaging

**For small businesses (yoga studios, dental offices):**
- Buyer: Administrative assistant or owner
- Knowledge: Might have heard of Salesforce, this might be first CRM, currently using spreadsheets
- Messaging: Broader about value ("keep all your client communication in one place")

**For midsize tech companies:**
- Buyer: VP of Sales well versed in CRMs
- Knowledge: Has used CRMs before, knows all high-level reasons
- Messaging: More specific about differentiation ("alerts you to opportunities" or "speeds up workflows with AI")

### Comparison

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Broader Messaging** | For people less aware/experienced. Highlights WHY solution matters. Typically encapsulates more than one feature. | "Always know where every project stands. Make legal a strategic part of the business, and stay way ahead of any issues." |
| **Specific Messaging** | For people who know a lot. Highlights differentiation. Often maps to a specific unique feature. | "Maintain a reliable, secure infrastructure to avoid disruptions and outages that slow you down." |

## The First Draft Is Sh*t

Ernest Hemingway said, "The first draft of anything is sh*t."

This is liberating:
- You're totally allowed to write a terrible first draft
- A garbage first draft is a natural part of the process

**The Agate Hunting Analogy:**

When hunting for agate on Oregon beaches, the pebbly sand looks gray and unremarkable. But when you put it through a sifter, beautiful clear agate appears. The gems were in the sand all along.

Your best ideas are like bright agate hiding in the gloopy gray sand of other thoughts. You have to toss a big clump on the page to sift through and find them.

**Give yourself permission to write long, crappy value props and benefits.** We'll refine them in the editing chapters.
