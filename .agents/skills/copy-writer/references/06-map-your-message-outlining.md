# Map Your Message: Drafting Your Outline

## Don't Skip the Outline

One of the most challenging parts of messaging is figuring out what to say that will resonate. If you haven't nailed that down before you start writing, you're liable to go into brown shaved ice territory. Trying to figure out WHAT you're saying and HOW you're saying it at the same time sends you down the wrong path.

### The Grocery Shopping Analogy

Without a list, you wander aimlessly through aisles, tossing random stuff (Oreos, broccoli, salted chocolate) that don't add up to a meal. Especially vulnerable to the siren song of snacks. The trip takes twice as long and costs twice as much.

When you get home, you still don't have everything you need to make a damn meal.

**This is what happens when you start writing messaging without knowing what to focus on.** You write aimlessly, toss in more words and ideas than needed—including junk—and none of it adds up to a clear, cohesive message.

## The Punchy Messaging Framework

Over the years, I've seen company and product messaging documents from all sorts of tech companies. They share one big problem: **way too much stuff inside them.** Field after field of value drivers, elevator pitches, benefits, capabilities, proof points, features, problem statements.

The most important parts—value proposition and benefits—get completely overlooked.

**The problem:** All this extra stuff distracts from building a clear perception in the market. When there are so many things on the menu, everyone orders something different, leading to inconsistencies.

### The Simple Solution: One Value Proposition + Three Benefits

```
VALUE PROPOSITION
├── Benefit 1 → Features
├── Benefit 2 → Features
└── Benefit 3 → Features
```

**That's it.** This minimalist approach helps you simplify what matters most, so colleagues and partners can easily repeat it.

### It Scales to Any Size

This framework works for messaging anything:
- Minor new product release
- Major platform launch
- Billion-dollar public company
- Early-stage startup

You can even nest them to create a full messaging house.

## Building Your Outline: 4 Steps

### Step 1: Look for Connections

Find connections between your customer's urgent pains and your product's best qualities.

**Example: Sales Engagement Platform**

| Urgent Pain | Product Quality |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Need salespeople to stop wasting precious selling time on data entry | Automates manual tasks so salespeople can focus on what matters |
| Need all reps to learn how to perform like my best reps | Identifies sales activities that work so you can roll out across team |
| Need to create a sales strategy based on data, not what we think works | Monitors and measures what's working in sales campaigns |

**Sales Director Example:**
- **Pain:** Needs entire sales team to hit targets. Almost everyone needs coaching. Loves helping team develop skills. Wishes he had more time.
- **Quality:** Coaching intelligence that monitors work and surfaces opportunities to improve. Pointers for quick coaching conversations.
- **Connection:** There's a clear link between what he wants (be a good coach, help team hit targets) and what product delivers (coaching insights).

### Step 2: Distill into Key Themes

Group similar and/or repeated connections into key themes.

**Example Themes:**

| Theme | Connections That Led to It |
|-------|---------------------------|
| Efficiency | Automating data entry, reducing manual tasks, saving reps time, focusing on activities proven to work |
| Visibility | Measuring sales activities, real-time view of rep performance, spotting issues early |

Some themes might slightly overlap ("better productivity" and "efficiency"). That's okay for now—look for main ideas.

### Step 3: Choose Your Value Proposition Theme

Find the theme that:
- Connects most clearly to the unique value you discovered
- Is echoed in some of your other key themes

**Don't worry about wordsmithing yet.** Just aim for a rough idea. If torn between two, put both down for now.

**Example possibilities:**
- "Easier sales"
- "Predictable revenue growth"

### Step 4: Choose Your Three Benefit Themes

Find three themes that:
- Directly underpin your value proposition
- Go a level deeper on HOW your product delivers the big win
- Don't dive into mechanics

**Rule:** The more unique AND critical a benefit, the more likely it should feature in messaging.

**Example benefit themes:**
1. More time for selling
2. Keep boosting performance
3. Scale what works

Then list the relevant features that deliver each benefit.

## Fiddling Until It Makes Sense

Like interior decorating, you have to move things around until they fit together and tell a cohesive story.

### Clarifying Questions

**Are you leading with the most important themes?**
The more important a message is to your customer, the higher it should feature. Your third benefit shouldn't be more critical than your value proposition. Put benefits in descending order of importance.

**Is the value proposition too vague?**
Sometimes the big win ends up too vague: "grow your business" or "save time and money." The more saturated your market, the more specific you need to be.

**Do your three benefits give a good overview?**
Does it feel like something is missing? You want to cover main points without sacrificing something key or differentiated. Sometimes you need to clarify a point; other times, rework the whole theme.

**Are your benefits related but distinct?**
Or are they blending together? "Productivity" and "performing daily tasks quicker" are too similar—the latter is a subset of the former. If two benefits have the exact same features below them, they're too similar.

## Tackling Larger Messaging Frameworks

For big platforms, product portfolios, or a master messaging house: **apply the VBF structure at different levels and nest them.**

### Example: Company Messaging House

1. **Company level:** VBF (one value prop, three benefits, key features/products)
2. **Product level:** Each product gets its own VBF
3. **Feature level:** Key features can get their own VBF if needed

### Example: Platform Messaging

1. **Platform level:** VBF for the platform
2. **Product level:** VBF for each individual product

### Adding Other Components

There's some room for Jobs to Be Done, customer proof points, etc. Just make sure value proposition and benefits remain front and center so messaging doesn't get lost.
