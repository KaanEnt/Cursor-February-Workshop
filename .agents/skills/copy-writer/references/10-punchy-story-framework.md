# Paint a Picture: The Punchy Story Framework

## Why Stories Matter

Whenever creating messaging for clients, a punchy story brings the value proposition and benefits to life. When you read it, you grasp the big picture and connect deeply with the customer's challenges and aspirations.

**The Science:** Your brain thinks stories are real. When you read a book or watch a movie, your mind automatically places you inside the action. You feel as if the hero's reality is your reality.

We can't help but get emotionally invested in a story. That's why we:
- Hang on every word of a friend's juicy gossip
- Feel pissed off when a movie ends without resolution
- Cry when a beloved character dies

**Stories make us care.**

## When to Use a Story

Not every messaging needs a story. Use this exercise when you're messaging something **big and important**:
- Your company
- A major product release
- Core website pages (Why Us?)
- First call deck opening
- Corporate videos, talks, campaigns

**Functional bonus:** A story gives colleagues and partners a clearer, richer understanding. They can read it once and get the full picture: customer challenges, how your product helps, why it matters. This helps them create effective materials that align with your message.

## The Punchy Story Framework

Inspired by Joseph Campbell's hero's journey from "The Hero with a Thousand Faces"—the twelve-stage plot structure in classic myths from Beowulf to Harry Potter.

**Critical reminder:** Your technology is NOT the hero of your story. That honor belongs to your customer.

The full hero's journey works for Hollywood trilogies, but we want punchy. Here are five simple phases:

```
1. The Shift
2. The Villain
3. Three Obstacles
4. The Dream
5. The Solution
```

## Phase 1: The Shift

Your story begins with a shift in your customer's world that forces them to make a change.

**Star Wars example:** Luke Skywalker finds out the Empire blew up his childhood home—an act of evil that forces him on a quest.

**Key characteristic:** Shifts can't be ignored. Your customer has no choice but to respond.

### Types of Shifts

| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| Global trend | Shift to remote work, changing consumer expectations |
| Change in how things are done | Moving to the cloud, digitization |
| Internal change | More pressure on team, seeking more purpose in work |

### Example: Slack (circa 2014)

**The shift:** Tech-focused teams were collaborating digitally and moving faster than ever before, but their current mode of communication couldn't keep pace.

This is super relatable for anyone working on a team, trying to communicate to get stuff done. It kicks off the story in the right arena of work and productivity.

## Phase 2: The Villain

Every story needs a villain. Without them: no conflict, no tension, no resolution. The story would be flat.

Villains put a face on the central problem. We see Darth Vader in all his evilness and shake our fists, rooting harder for Luke.

**The villain emerges from the shift.** It puts a face on the core problem your product is solving, likely connected to your one big win.

### Types of Villains

| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| An object | Spreadsheet, sticky notes |
| An approach | Using point solutions as temporary bandages |
| A concept | Blind spots in customer service, complexity |
| A person | A hacker |

### Example: Slack

**The villain:** Email.

Old-fashioned email was getting in the way of productivity because:
- Took too long for people to respond
- Things got lost in threads
- You could never find things fast enough

Email is a great villain because many people could relate to its problems—and these were the people most likely to enjoy Slack.

## Phase 3: Three Obstacles

The villain tries to thwart the hero by throwing obstacles in their path. Darth Vader used Stormtroopers and fighter planes to stop Luke.

**Obstacles are concrete, distinct subproblems** that trickle from the bigger problem.

### Types of Obstacles

| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| Objects and things | Non-stop alerts |
| Daily occurrences | Breakdowns in manual processes |
| Emotions | Feeling unsure, lacking confidence |

### Example: Slack

The three obstacles created by email:

1. **Can't find what you need to get work done** → Maps to Slack's benefit of searching and finding anything quickly
2. **Missing out on stuff with siloed communication** → Maps to Slack's benefit of organized group conversations
3. **Replies are taking too long** → Maps to Slack's benefit of messaging faster than email

**Notice:** Obstacles map to your three benefits. They show why the hero can't live with the villain.

## Phase 4: The Dream

Dreams inspire and motivate us to overcome challenges. The dream is how your customer wishes things would be, if that darn villain and his obstacles weren't in the way.

**Luke Skywalker's dream:** Peace in the galaxy. That's what got him out of bed each morning.

### Two Parts of the Dream

| Part | Example |
|------|---------|
| The tangible thing or outcome | 24/7 threat protection they can count on |
| How they'll feel when it happens | Peace of mind |

**The truth:** We often desire "things" but what we really want is the feeling we'll have once that thing is in our possession. That's why both parts matter.

**Example:** Your customer may say they want to automate boring, manual tasks. But really, they want to make a bigger, more strategic contribution to the business.

### Example: Slack

**The dream:** Being more productive—without wasting time on admin and other busywork.

## Phase 5: The Solution

Your product finally enters to provide a helping hand.

**Luke's solution:** The Force. He used it to overcome obstacles and defeat Darth Vader to achieve peace in the galaxy.

**Note:** The Force was a tool, but not the thing that defeated the villain. It was LUKE, backed by the superpower of the Force, who got the job done.

### The Same Applies to Your Product

Your software is NOT the savior riding in on a trusty steed. It's the magic weapon the customer needs to overcome everything.

This section:
- Connects the dots between your unique delivery and the dream
- Highlights the value proposition and three benefits
- Ends with the dream

### Example: Slack

**The solution:** Making work simpler, more pleasant, and more productive by bringing together all team communication in one place with real-time messaging, archive, and search.

**Notice:** It's high level. No diving into features and how it works. Just show how benefits and value proposition address the villain and obstacles.

## Crafting Your Story: Paragraph by Paragraph

### Structure

| Paragraph | Content |
|-----------|---------|
| 1 | Sets up the shift |
| 2 | Introduces the villain and obstacles |
| 3 | Connects to the customer's dream |
| 4 | Presents your solution |

Write from the customer's perspective using second-person "you" to feel immediate and human. (Can also write third-person if more appropriate.)

### Example: Chatty (AI Customer Support Chatbot)

**Hero:** Head of customer experience

---

**Paragraph 1: Set Up the Shift**

State the shift and how it's directly affecting the customer. Don't spend too long on the preamble—quickly connect the macro shift to how it shows up in their life. **No more than 3 sentences.**

*The shift:* How modern consumers expect to interact with brands. Brands used to dictate when and where customers could get in touch. Now, consumers hold the power and demand instant support or they'll take their issue to social media.

> Today's customers come at you from every angle 24/7—they're on every channel, and they want answers and help now (like, now now).

---

**Paragraph 2: Introduce the Villain and Obstacles**

Connect to un-ignorable problems your customer will recognize and relate to. Describe problems in the context of their life. **No more than 5 sentences.**

*The villain:* A growing mountain of support desk tickets. The highest motivating pain for prospects, the reason they start looking for a solution.

*The obstacles:*
1. Existing team is overworked, focused on low-value work
2. Constantly having to hire and onboard new reps
3. Risk to brand reputation

> But your customer support team can't be everywhere, solving every issue. And they don't want to either—who likes answering the exact same question two hundred times a day? Your team is built to handle your customers' toughest problems, not track a package or find a knowledge base article. But you can't hire and train enough live agents to keep up with all the questions—and keep the social media trolls at bay.

---

**Paragraph 3: Connect to the Dream**

Introduce how they wish things were going differently. Brings emotional side to benefits. **No more than 3 sentences.**

*The dream:* Make sure every customer gets the right resolution as quickly as possible—ideally, without always talking to the support team. So the support team can focus on requests that need their expertise.

> You want to help customers find answers fast and walk away with a smile. You want your team engaged when real expertise is the only way to get the job done. You need a way to make your support team's job easier, not harder.

---

**Paragraph 4: Present Your Solution**

Introduce value proposition and three benefits. End with why it matters in the big picture. **Don't go into features or how it works—just enough to clarify what the solution is and what it can do.**

*Value proposition:* Provide the kind of support customers want with your existing team.

*Benefits:*
1. Provide immediate 24/7 support
2. Free up agents to be more strategic
3. Deliver a better customer experience

> Provide the modern customer support your consumers demand with Chatty's customer support bot. It engages your customers so seamlessly they won't miss a human agent, but it's ready to find one if someone needs it. Customers leave with the answers they need and a great impression of your brand. With Chatty, you can give your customers a conversation that really says something.

## Clarifying Questions

**Are your customers aware of the villain and obstacles?**
They should be obvious and undeniable. Things customers have already identified as issues. If you see an underlying problem that isn't clear to them, meet customers where they're at.

**Does your villain potentially offend someone?**
Don't mistake the villain for someone doing something negative. One client cited their own sales team as the villain! Choose a villain that unites your organization rather than divides it. Like a giant Excel spreadsheet.

**Is the shift too big, vague, and/or a cliché?**
"The world has changed" is a no-brainer. The world is constantly changing. Start there when brainstorming, but keep going. Then see if you can start later in the story. Make it very connected to your customer.

**Does your solution paragraph dive into technical weeds?**
You can't start a story about feelings and challenges, then dive straight into machine learning models. It's too big a leap. The goal is to grasp high-level messaging.

## Using the Story to Refine Messaging

Sometimes this exercise brings up new angles to improve your messaging framework.

### Case Study: Email Security Company

**Original focus:** Platform's ability to stop impersonation attacks.

**During story exercise:** Realized the main villain wasn't the hacker (too big). The villain was traditional user training courses that told employees what to do with suspicious emails. Once-a-year training wasn't enough, which is why breaches continued.

**The insight:** This was a unique, tangible villain they could really tackle—unlike all the bad hackers in the world.

**The result:** Originally a benefit, this idea was elevated to the value proposition because it was so tied to differentiation. The platform stood out from others bragging about "market-leading" tech.

---

**This is the easiest (and most fun) exercise in the book.** Picture each phase like a blockbuster movie. Imagine scenes for your customer. Amp up the drama! Even if the brainstorm goes nowhere, spending time imagining life as your customer is worthwhile.
