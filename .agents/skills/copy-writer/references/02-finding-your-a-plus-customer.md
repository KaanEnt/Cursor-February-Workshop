# Finding Your A+ Customer

## The White Elephant Gift Exchange Problem

You can't choose the right gift for a mystery person because you have no clue what they want. Just like you can't expect to create the right messaging for a mystery buyer. If you don't know who they are, every sentence you write is a shot in the dark.

## Write for One Person to Resonate with a Crowd

Figuring out who you're talking to in B2B tech isn't straightforward. You're likely dealing with multiple audiences, personas, and stakeholders. This brings up the natural urge to try to speak to everyone.

**Unfortunately, the mythical "one message that will rule them all" doesn't exist.**

Every audience has their own challenges, needs, and wants. When you try to cover them all, you strip out important details. And when you try to appeal to everyone inside an organization, your messaging ends up sounding like it's written for a corporate entity.

**But businesses don't read your messaging. People do.**

### The Dog Whistle Effect

When you write for one specific buyer, your messaging stands out like a dog whistle. People around you wouldn't notice, but every dog would have their ears pricked. Similarly, when you write for one specific buyer, you draw them in by speaking about what matters to them. Person by person, you effectively reach a larger crowd than you'd ever reach with a generic message.

## Who Is Your A+ Customer?

Your A+ customer is a raving fan of your product. They love it so much that they refer your product to others without being asked.

### Questions to Identify Them

**Who are your happiest customers?**
Look for the types of customers who love your product and often refer it to others. Who churns less and renews more?

**Who gets the most value from your product?**
Your product solves lots of problems, but some are worth paying more for. Which customers see your product as a must-have? Who needs your product the most?

**Who are the super users of your product?**
A+ customers tend to be aligned with your product vision. Look for customers who are using more sophisticated features and influencing your roadmap.

## Case Study: Cash Flow Management Platform

A cash flow management platform had been growing for five years by selling to heads of finance at all kinds of companies: global enterprises, multinational businesses, and high-growth companies. They weren't sure what to talk about in their new messaging.

They had lots of customers, but not all were A+ customers. Quite a few were C- customers: traditional multinational businesses that were hard to sell to, thought the platform was too expensive, and only used basic features.

**Their A+ customers were the opposite:** Private equity-backed, high-growth companies that needed advanced control over their cash flow. The platform's value was clear to them, deals moved faster, and they signed without balking at price.

By tailoring messaging for the finance lead in this type of business, they attracted other like-minded buyers who wanted that too. They began to attract multinational businesses that aspired to be like their high-growth A+ customers.

## The Fear of Narrowing

Almost every company grapples with this fear. Speaking to fewer people feels at odds with selling to more customers.

**But here's the paradox:** Focusing on your A+ customers doesn't automatically slam the door on other types. It has a halo effect that extends to other like-minded buyers.

## FAQ: Common A+ Customer Questions

### What If I Have Different Audiences?

Even if your product serves multiple audiences and job titles, focus on ONE A+ customer in your messaging.

- **Early-stage startup:** Critical to gain traction. Double down on one customer type to become the obvious choice.
- **Larger company with multiple audiences:** Write separate messaging for each, but for top-level messaging, still focus on the A+ customer.

### Is My A+ Customer the User, Buyer, or Decision-Maker?

Even in a buying group with 6-10 people, you still have to pick a person to focus on.

**Don't automatically focus on the C-suite.** Just because the CEO or CTO is in your buying group doesn't mean your messaging should totally focus on them. They're probably not the ones hunting for solutions or reading your website. They rely on colleagues to make recommendations.

**Focus on the champion:** The person motivated to push the solution through. They're doing the legwork of researching options and reading your messaging.

### What If I'm Trying to Reach a Different Customer?

Sometimes your A+ customers aren't enough to support growth targets. Consider a phased approach:

**Example:** A fintech company's A+ customers were bootstrapped startup founders. They wanted to go upmarket to heads of finance at venture-backed startups.

Both wanted the same things: access to capital and daily efficiencies. The key difference was heads of finance also cared about scaling operations. They focused on shared needs in the majority of messaging, and addressed scaling in one benefit. This supported expansion without alienating existing customers.

### What If My Startup Has No Customers Yet?

Make your best guess based on available data:
- What kind of customer would you gladly have?
- Who has the problem your product solves?
- Who would get the most value?

Writing for a defined customer, even if it's your best guess, is more effective than writing for everyone. It gives you a truer read on whether it resonates with your potential target.

## Beware the Curse of Knowledge

The Curse of Knowledge is a cognitive bias that happens when you know a lot about a subject. You can't remember what it was like to not know all that stuff. You unconsciously assume everyone else knows what you know.

This is why so much tech messaging goes over people's heads.

### Questions to Gauge Customer Knowledge

**How Tech-Savvy Are They?**
There are technical buyers and business buyers, and a spectrum between. Get clear on how technical (or not) your A+ customers are.

**How Aware of Your Solution Are They?**
Do they have a good idea what your software does, or is this the first time they're learning about a product like yours?

- **Mature category (like CRM):** Strong understanding of what the solution does
- **New/emerging category:** Might not even know a solution like yours exists

Knowing where your A+ customer is in relation to your tech will help shape your messaging.
