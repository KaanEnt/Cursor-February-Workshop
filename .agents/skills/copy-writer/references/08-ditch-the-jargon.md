# Ditch the Jargon

## The Carrot-Based Workflow Story

In a messaging workshop, the CMO kept talking about "carrot-based workflows" as a key driver of productivity. It sounded odd. First vegetable reference in a product messaging workshop.

Everyone in the Zoom call was nodding along like they knew what it meant. Google only brought up cooking recipes.

Finally: "This may be a dumb question, but what is a carrot-based workflow?"

The CMO smiled and explained it was a term his team made up. "I've been using it for a while and I really like it."

A colleague DMed: "Thank god you asked. I didn't know what he was talking about either!!"

**His jargon was supposed to articulate unique value around developer productivity. Instead, it confused everyone and made a few feel dumb.**

This is happening on a global scale in tech, thanks to jargon.

## Jargon: The Sworn Enemy of Clear Messaging

Jargon is the number one obstacle between technology and messaging that resonates with actual humans. This language tries to sound impressive but doesn't say anything at all.

**Example:** "Leveraging best-in-class technology to transform business outcomes."

Sounds like one business robot talking to other business robots.

### Jargon Is Like Refined Sugar

At first, it feels like a sweet way to communicate power and innovation. You can quickly get addicted. But they're empty calories that bloat your message with meaningless words.

**The negative effects:**

1. **Hides the real value of your product.** Tricks you into thinking you've conveyed something big when you haven't said much of anything.

2. **Creates skepticism and mistrust.** Do you believe big, hyped statements about revolutionary products? Neither do your buyers. One study showed jargon caused confusion and/or dislike in 49% of audiences.

3. **Makes you sound like everyone else.** Instead of highlighting what makes you unique, jargon makes you blend into a crowded market.

## The Four Flavors of Jargon

### 1. Corporate-Speak

**Examples:** leverage, synergy, bandwidth, agility

Thrown around so casually in B2B that it's easy to overlook. (You've probably said "bandwidth" five times this week.) We started using these to sound "professional" and "serious." But fancy corporate-y words make you sound like a robot and don't add clarity or spice.

### 2. Buzzwords and Hype

**Examples:** transform, revolutionize, supercharge, game-changing, reimagine

Many tech brands lean on these to communicate power and innovation. They've lost almost all meaning. Way too focused on glorifying technology and ignoring the customer. And that's boring.

### 3. Internal Lingo

**Examples:** end-to-end ecosystem, composable automation, carrot-based workflows

Words and phrases teams make up themselves. Get bounced around in the internal echo chamber until they seem like they'll resonate with the market.

**But they don't.** Your customers aren't in your head or team meetings. They're mystified—you spend precious time explaining what your made-up word is trying to say.

### 4. Deep Tech Talk

**Examples:** augmented analytics, downstream systems, ML-defined data abstraction

Advanced technical language only an expert or day-to-day practitioner would understand. Most B2B messaging addresses a buying group of 6-10 people. Only 1-2 will be enticed by technical language. The rest—business buyers and decision-makers focused on results and value—will be confused and bored.

## How to De-Jargon Your Messaging

### Step 1: Hunt for Jargon

Review your messaging and look for jargon, from the glaringly obvious "synergy" to the questionable "streamline."

### Step 2: Translate What It Actually Means

Trade out jargon for simple, human language that speaks to real benefits. Use everyday words. Help readers picture it in their mind.

**Example:**

| Jargon | Translation |
|--------|-------------|
| "Achieve operational excellence" | "Deliver consistent service" |

What does "achieving operational excellence" look like through their eyes? To them, it could simply mean their business can deliver consistent service.

**More Examples:**

| Before (Jargon) | After (Translation) |
|-----------------|---------------------|
| Unleash the power of 5G with best-of-breed, multigenerational network infrastructure | Move fast with 5G while preserving the value of your 3G and 4G networks |
| Leverage over a dozen data destinations simultaneously | Use 12+ destinations at once |
| Enable customers to run analytics on data stored in MySQL databases | Run analytics on data stored in MySQL databases |

## "But My A+ Customer Uses Jargon Too"

There IS a nugget of truth here.

Don't go out of your way to avoid industry terms familiar to your readers just because it sounds like jargon to your neighbor or mom.

**Examples that are probably fine:**
- "Ransomware" and "zero-day" for threat protection
- "Talent acquisition" for recruiting solutions
- "APIs" instead of "application programming interfaces"

You need a sprinkle of these terms to frame your solution and build credibility.

**But:** Is your reader using deep tech talk ALL the time? Probably not. Use industry terms as needed for accuracy, but always remember you're connecting with a human who wants to hear that you understand their problem.

### The "Is It Jargon?" Test

Ask two questions:

1. **Will it be universally understood by your audience?**
2. **Is it meaningful to your audience?**

**Example: "Omnichannel"**

For martech solution aimed at B2B marketers:
- Universally understood? Yes
- Meaningful? Yes
- **Include it.**

**Example: "Accelerating business outcomes"**

For those same B2B marketers:
- Understood? Sort of (some think revenue, others think efficiency)
- Meaningful? No clear meaning
- **Find a clearer way.**

## Keep a Jargon Blacklist

When working with a company struggling to break up with jargon, give them a blacklist of words to avoid. There are always common offenders. This makes it easier to identify them without figuring it out themselves.

Eventually, these words won't even try to creep back—they'll know they aren't welcome.

## Coined Terms and Category Names

In 2005, HubSpot's Brian Halligan coined "inbound marketing." The company became synonymous with a movement that changed marketing forever.

Since then, many tech companies have tried to do the same, spawning a whole new wave of jargon.

**The reality:** With so many competing solutions, it's impossible for everyone to create their own movement. Behind every enviable success story are countless unmentioned failures. Many teams have had to abandon coined names because they never took off—or worse—confused customers.

### Clarity Over Coining

**Recommendation:** Consider clarity over coining. The goal of messaging is to help people quickly grasp your unique value. Most of the time, simple, straightforward language serves you better than a made-up name.

**Example: Cybersecurity Company**

They coined "attacker-centric behavioral analytics" for their technical differentiation. It wasn't landing—too jargony, hard to understand, impossible to remember.

**In customer interviews,** customers described it their own way: "risk-based blocking." It described the outcome, not the technology. The words weren't as impressive, but when people heard it, they intuitively got what it meant.

**Compare:** Decode your meaning vs. intuitive understanding.

## Convincing Leadership to Banish Jargon

For many companies, jargon is so deeply ingrained that fighting it feels like a losing battle. You strip out jargon, a colleague writes it back in. You try an interesting approach, you're asked to add jargon to sound "more enterprise."

### The Business Case

**Real improvement in marketing campaigns.** One team applied what they learned to paid campaigns and saw a huge lift because it was human and personal—not the same robotic language.

**Perceived as a modern company.** A website full of jargon is a legacy approach. Buyers want to connect with authentic companies. Asana, Slack, Dropbox, Stripe have proven you don't need to sound like a droid.

**Value becomes accessible.** "A confused mind doesn't buy." A confused mind says "Yikes. I can't deal with all that" and moves on.

**You'll stand out.** Using jargon makes you sound like everyone else. Clear, human messaging stands out, especially in technical arenas like cybersecurity, IT, enterprise solutions. One cybersecurity firm reduced jargon on their homepage and increased demo sign-ups.

### Case Study: Company-Wide Jargon Elimination

One CMO hired Punchy to teach his team of 300 product marketers how to write simple, jargon-free messaging.

**Results:**
- No argument whether people can use technical jargon
- Everyone knows why simple messaging matters
- Fewer back-and-forth revisions
- Messaging is just plain better

**This worked because it came from the top.** Going cold turkey as a group is the path to fewer rewrites and better connection with buyers.
