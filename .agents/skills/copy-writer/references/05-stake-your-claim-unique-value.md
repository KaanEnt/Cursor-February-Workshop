# Stake Your Claim: Finding Your Unique Value

## The Brown Shaved Ice Problem

A shaved ice truck lets kids self-serve from fourteen syrup nozzles: lemon, dragon fruit, root beer, cherry, blue raspberry. With so many good choices, kids put a little bit of everything in their cup.

The assumption: all those awesome flavors will bond together into mega-awesomeness.

The reality: colors slump to a murky bottom. A brown shaved ice that tastes the way it looks. After a few bites, it gets thrown away.

**There's a brown shaved ice equivalent in messaging.** Too many different benefits and ideas swirled together until it gets murky and hard to swallow.

### Example: Brown Shaved Ice Messaging

A cybersecurity platform homepage with five different benefits in one thirty-five-word sentence. By the end, you've forgotten the beginning and can't tell what the product does.

### Why Companies Do This

Fear of missing something important. Fear a potential buyer might want to hear something else. So they talk about efficiency, risk reduction, AND cost savings. All in one breath. Just to cover all bases.

**Instead:** Have faith in what your product can uniquely deliver to your A+ customer—and give it the room it deserves.

## Your Unique Value Is Your Messaging Hook

Your product's unique value is the **number one reason a buyer should choose your product over others.**

### What Unique Value Is

- A main idea, not a customer-facing message
- The ONE thing you'd like your product to become known for
- Something you must repeat over and over, metaphorically whacking people over the head

### What Unique Value Is NOT

- A subtle differentiator
- A list of several good things
- Something competitors could easily claim

### Example: Online Learning Platform

A popular platform offered hands-on training for engineers. All competitors talked about the same things: thousands of courses, expert instructors, exams, certifications, simple admin.

**Their unique approach:** Unlike theory-led courses, their teaching was hands-on and practical. Students applied techniques alongside instructors, resulting in faster, deeper learning. And the founders believed learning should be fun.

**Their unique value:** "Learning by doing"

**Results:**
- Highlighted in value proposition and drilled into benefits
- Within months, synonymous with "learning by doing"
- Built perception as the top hands-on learning provider
- Competitors started copying the language, but couldn't shift market perception
- Year-over-year growth continued

## Three Perspectives to Find Unique Value

### 1. Your Company's Perspective: What's at the Core?

Your product's unique value often connects directly to your company or product vision—why you built it in the first place.

**Questions:**
- What's the core problem you're solving?
- What makes your way of solving it unique?
- What would you be abandoning if you chased competitor messaging?

### Case Study: Cybersecurity Company

A cybersecurity company had a remarkable approach around protecting APIs and applications. For years, they found growth with security people in midsize companies who knew they needed protection but didn't have expertise or time to manage it themselves.

**Their offering:** Proactive software and service that took the task—and worry—off their plate completely.

**The pressure:** New startups in adjacent technology were getting funding and attention, speaking more to developers. The company felt pressure to compete against them too.

**The identity crisis:** Caught between who they were and who they felt they should be.

**The resolution:** Permission to realize their unique value was what they always set out to be: great technology with services included, so people could get advanced protection without worrying. They gave people their nights and weekends back.

**The result:** They doubled down and grew their existing customer base even faster.

### 2. Your Customers' Perspective: What Do They Routinely Rave About?

Anything customers always say they love is a good indicator of unique value.

### Case Study: Endpoint Management Company

They were in a highly technical space, up against big software companies with classic messaging about robust features, AI, and innovation. But the power of those solutions came at a cost of complexity. IT teams wasted hours trying to manage them.

**What customers loved:** Like hitting "the easy button." Get up and running in minutes, don't need a manual, and it gets the job done. Every customer talked about it.

**The hesitation:** Part of them worried that "easy to use" would make them look simplistic.

**The reality:** It's what every customer wanted.

**The result:** "The easy button solution" became their unique value. They quadrupled growth after two quarters.

### 3. Your Competitors' Perspective: What Aren't They Talking About?

**Competitive Messaging Audit:**

Create a table with your top five competitors (the ones that most often come up in sales calls):

| Company | Unique Value (headline) | Main Messages (prominent benefits) |
|---------|------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| Competitor 1 | ... | ... |
| Competitor 2 | ... | ... |
| Competitor 3 | ... | ... |
| Competitor 4 | ... | ... |
| Competitor 5 | ... | ... |

**What to look for:**
- Repetition and well-worn themes to avoid
- Anything NOT being said (potential opportunity)

**Caution:** Don't get too swept up in what competitors say. Your unique value shouldn't be wholly defined by competitor language. They don't have it all figured out, and they don't own particular words.

## Choosing Just One Thing

If you have multiple themes emerging, you must decide which unique value to use.

### The Fast Method

Ask yourself: "If we could be known for just one of these things, which one should it be?"

This forces you to decide based on:
- Product vision
- What customers think
- What the rest of the market is saying

### The Slower Method

Remove themes one by one:
1. Start with all your themes
2. Ask which one you can safely take away
3. Remove it, reassess
4. Repeat until you're down to one

### Case Study: Heavyweight vs Lightweight

An integration platform was incredibly robust. Technical buyers loved the heavyweight solution with in-depth features for large, global enterprises.

**The pressure:** Younger, "cooler" startups were talking about lightweight, no-code solutions any business user could master.

**The realization:** You can't be both heavyweight AND lightweight. Like a person can't be tall and short at the same time.

**The question:** "Which one feels most true to your platform, and who you are as a company?"

**The answer:** They were the heavyweight solution. It was who they'd always been, why they were the darling of technical buyers.

**The unique value:** Solving the complexity of enterprise integrations.

## Remember

- Your product was built to solve a special problem in a special way—that's often where unique value lies
- Choosing one idea doesn't mean you can never mention other nice things
- Other benefits may be expressed in benefits or live in sales/marketing assets for people further in the buying cycle
- The role of messaging is to open the first door and invite folks in to learn more

## Case Study: Loom Doubles Down on Async Work

During the pandemic, millions turned to Loom for a new way to communicate in the remote world. They were ready for new messaging to support rapid growth and differentiate from other solutions.

**The challenge:** So many valuable use cases and benefits, it was hard to focus on one unique difference.

**The approach:** Looked at what was core to Loom's vision, what customers raved about, and how competitors talked.

**The golden thread:** Loom gave people a better way to work together asynchronously.
- Customers loved sending a Loom instead of having a live meeting
- Distributed teams could work across time zones faster
- Companies became more productive in the new distributed world

**The opportunity:** In early 2021, "async work" was still a budding concept. Loom could claim it.

**The messaging:** Headlines like "meetings off, Loom on" made it clear how Loom could help.

**The result:** As async work grew more popular, so did Loom. Now synonymous with replacing meetings, an essential tool for modern work:
- 25 million+ users
- 400,000+ companies
- ~1.5 billion minutes recorded
