# Ogilvy's Research Methodology

> "I am helpless without research material." — David Ogilvy

## The Foundation: Gallup Training

Ogilvy spent years at George Gallup's Audience Research Institute, conducting over 400 nationwide surveys. This experience cured him of reliance on intuition.

**Key insight:** What people *say* they like often differs from what they *buy*.

---

## The Three Roles of Research

### 1. Measuring Reputation

Understand how the brand is currently perceived before trying to change it.

**Questions to answer:**
- What do customers currently believe about the brand?
- What associations does the brand have?
- Where does the brand sit relative to competitors?

### 2. Product Testing

Determine if the product is actually good.

> "Good advertising can kill a bad product faster because it exposes the fault to more people more quickly."

**Ogilvy's rule:** He refused to advertise bad products. The advertising only works if the product delivers on the promise.

### 3. Concept Testing

Before spending millions on media, test promises and headlines.

**Method:**
- Show different concepts to consumers
- Measure which one elicits the highest purchase intent
- Let the market decide, not the creative team's opinion

---

## The "Promise" Test

Ogilvy believed the most important decision was:
1. How should I position the product?
2. What is the promise?

### Testing Different Promises

For a Helena Rubinstein face cream, he might test:
- "Makes you look younger"
- "Cleans deep into pores"
- "Recommended by dermatologists"

The winner (measured by consumer response) became the campaign.

---

## Competitor Analysis

Before writing, Ogilvy studied the precedents:
- All previous advertising for the product category
- What worked and what failed
- What competitors are currently claiming

**Purpose:** Stand on the shoulders of giants rather than reinvent the wheel.

---

## The Research Process for Headlines

### Step 1: Study the Product

Ogilvy spent three weeks studying Rolls-Royce before writing a single word:
- Technical reports
- Engineering manuals
- Customer testimonials
- Competitor comparisons

### Step 2: Find the Facts

Look for specific, surprising facts that demonstrate quality rather than claim it.

**The Rolls-Royce discovery:** Buried in a technical editor's report from *The Motor* magazine, he found: "At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock."

### Step 3: Test the Headlines

Write multiple versions. Test them:
- Against each other (A/B testing)
- With target consumers
- For recall and purchase intent

---

## Research Informs Targeting

### The Flag Technique

Research tells you WHO to flag in the headline:
- Who are the actual buyers?
- What do they call themselves?
- What problems keep them up at night?

### Audience Profiling

Ogilvy learned from selling Aga cookers door-to-door:
- Know the prospect's profession, wealth, and hobbies
- The arguments used to sell to a stockbroker (investment return) differ from those used to sell to a doctor (hygiene and health)

---

## What Research Cannot Do

Research tells you what works, but it cannot:
- Create the Big Idea (that requires creative leap)
- Replace judgment (interpret data, don't be enslaved by it)
- Guarantee success (only improve probabilities)

> "While other copywriters waited for the muse, Ogilvy looked at the data."

---

## Applying Research to Modern Headlines

### Email Subject Lines

- Test different subject lines (A/B testing)
- Measure open rates as the "purchase intent" equivalent
- Learn what language your specific audience responds to

### Content Headlines

- Use analytics to see what headlines drive clicks
- Study the headlines of high-performing content in your space
- Test variations before committing to a final version

### Landing Pages

- Heat maps show what readers look at
- Scroll depth shows how far they read
- Conversion rates reveal which headlines actually work

---

## The Research Mindset

1. **Never assume you know the answer** - Test it
2. **Collect facts obsessively** - The Big Idea often hides in research
3. **Let data trump opinion** - When research contradicts intuition, trust research
4. **Study what worked before** - Learn from history before innovating
