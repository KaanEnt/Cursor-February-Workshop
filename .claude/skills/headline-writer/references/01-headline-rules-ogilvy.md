# Ogilvy's Headline Rules

> "On the average, five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy. When you have written your headline, you have spent eighty cents out of your dollar." — David Ogilvy

## The Economics of Attention

Ogilvy viewed reader attention as currency. If the headline fails to sell, 80% of the client's money is wasted regardless of how brilliant the body copy is.

**Process implication:** Ogilvy would write 20, 50, or even 100 headlines for a single ad before selecting the winner, often testing them against one another in split-run tests.

---

## The Four Functions of a Headline

Every headline must perform at least one of these functions:

### 1. Select the Prospect (The Flag)

The headline should act as a flag to the target audience. If you are selling to mothers, use the word "Mothers" in the headline. If you are selling a tour of Europe, mention "Europe."

**Warning:** Avoid "blind" headlines that try to be tricky or obscure. They simply get skipped by the people who might actually buy.

**Examples:**
- "To men who want to quit work someday"
- "Advice for wives whose husbands don't save money"
- "For engineers who want to advance their careers"

### 2. Promise a Benefit

The headline must answer the consumer's primary question: "What's in it for me?" It should clearly state what the product will do for the reader.

**Key insight:** Appeal to self-interest. The reader cares about themselves, not your product.

### 3. Deliver News

Consumers are always on the lookout for what is new. Headlines containing news are consistently effective.

**Power words for news:** New, Now, Announcing, Introducing, Finally, At Last, Suddenly

### 4. Offer Service or Helpful Information

Headlines that teach (e.g., "How to...") attract readers because they offer immediate value independent of the purchase.

## Technical Parameters

### Include the Brand Name

You must mention the brand in the headline. If the reader scans the page and misses the body copy, they should at least know *who* is advertising. Failing to do so wastes the impression.

### Length Matters

Ogilvy found that long headlines often outsold short ones. Headlines of 10 words or longer tend to sell more goods than short, punchy ones.

**Reasoning:** A long headline can convey a complete selling message, whereas a short one often cannot.

**Example:** "At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock" (19 words)

### Specifics over Generalities

Avoid "best in the world." Use specific facts.

| Vague | Specific |
|-------|----------|
| "A quiet car" | "At 60 miles an hour, the loudest noise comes from the electric clock" |
| "Save money" | "Save $500 on your energy bill" |
| "Fast delivery" | "Delivered in 2 hours or less" |

### Use Quotation Marks

Headlines in quotation marks increase recall by 28%. They imply that someone is speaking, adding a conversational and human element.

---

## What to Avoid

### Puns and Wordplay

Ogilvy hated "tricky" headlines. Clarity sells; cleverness confuses.

> "I don't want people to say 'What a clever ad.' I want them to say 'I never knew that. I must try this product.'"

### Literary Allusions

References that require cultural knowledge exclude readers and obscure the message.

### Obscurity

The headline must telegraph the message instantly. If the reader has to work to understand it, they won't.

---

## The Headline Checklist

Before finalizing a headline, verify:

- [ ] Does it promise a benefit?
- [ ] Does it contain news (if applicable)?
- [ ] Does it flag the specific prospect?
- [ ] Is the brand name included?
- [ ] Is it specific rather than general?
- [ ] Is it free of puns and wordplay?
- [ ] Can it be understood instantly?
- [ ] Would it work in quotation marks?

## Famous Ogilvy Headlines (Study These)

### Rolls-Royce
"At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock."
- **Why it works:** Specific fact, implies quality without saying "quality," 19 words but every one earns its place

### Dove
"Dove creams your skin while you wash."
- **Why it works:** Clear benefit, differentiates from soap, simple promise

### Puerto Rico
"Now Puerto Rico Offers 100% Tax Exemption to New Industry."
- **Why it works:** News hook ("Now"), specific benefit (100%), flags the prospect (industry)

### Hathaway
"The man in the Hathaway shirt."
- **Why it works:** Story appeal, curiosity (the eyepatch in the visual), aspirational identity
