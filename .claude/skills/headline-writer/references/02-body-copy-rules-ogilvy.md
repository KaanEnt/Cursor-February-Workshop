# Ogilvy's Body Copy Rules

> "The more you tell, the more you sell." — David Ogilvy

## The "Letter" Mindset

Write as if you are writing each reader a letter. The tone should be personal, warm, and direct.

**Avoid:** Addressing the crowd ("People of America!")
**Instead:** Write to one person, as if having a private conversation between two intelligent people.

---

## Long Copy vs. Short Copy

Ogilvy was a staunch defender of **long copy**.

### The Logic

If a consumer is genuinely interested in a product (a luxury car, a stove, a vacation), they are hungry for information. They want to justify the purchase. Short copy leaves their questions unanswered.

### The Nuance

Long copy is not an excuse for boring copy. It must be:
- Fascinating
- Factual
- Respectful of the reader's time by providing value in every sentence

### Readership Pattern

While readership drops after 50 words, it stabilizes between 50 and 500 words. The "qualified" prospects—the ones who will actually buy—read the long copy.

---

## Factual Content over "Puffery"

Ogilvy detested adjectives that conveyed no information.

### Words to Avoid

- "Top-quality"
- "Premier"
- "World-class"
- "Best-in-class"
- "Revolutionary" (without evidence)

### The Alternative

Instead of saying it, describe it:
- Instead of "safe," describe the safety features
- Instead of "fast," give the 0-60 time
- Instead of "reliable," cite the uptime percentage

> "Factual advertising like this outsells flatulent puffery."

## The "Reason Why" Technique

Inherited from Claude Hopkins, this technique involves stating a claim and immediately backing it up with a rational justification.

### Structure

**Claim + Because + Fact**

### Examples

| Product | Claim | Reason Why |
|---------|-------|------------|
| Dove | "Dove will not dry your skin" | "because it contains 1/4 cleansing cream" |
| Rolls-Royce | "The quietest car at highway speed" | "because of the triple muffler system and 7-hour engine run-in" |

### Why It Works

A claim alone triggers a defense mechanism. A claim with a reason triggers a logic processing mechanism. It respects the consumer's intelligence by offering evidence rather than just hyperbole.

## Structural Techniques (The "Bucket Brigade")

To keep the reader moving through long copy:

### Short Sentences and Paragraphs

Long blocks of text are intimidating. Short paragraphs (sometimes just one sentence) invite the eye to continue.

### Subheads

Breaking up text with intriguing subheads keeps the reader engaged and allows skimmers to get the gist.

### Drop Caps

Using a large initial capital letter to start the body copy increases readership by drawing the eye down from the headline.

### Captions Under Photos

**Critical insight:** Twice as many people read the captions under photographs as read the body copy.

**Rules:**
- Never use a photograph without a caption
- The caption must be a miniature advertisement
- It should contain the brand name and the promise
- If the reader only reads the caption, they should still get the sales pitch

---

## Plain Language

Write in the language of the customer.

> "Don't use highfalutin language... I once used the word OBSOLETE in a headline, only to discover that 43% of housewives had no idea what it meant."

### Word Swap Reference

| Instead of... | Try... |
|---------------|--------|
| Utilize | Use |
| Implement | Do |
| Facilitate | Make easier |
| Leverage | Use |
| Optimize | Improve |
| Ensure | Make sure |
| Mitigate | Reduce |

---

## Layout and Typography Rules

### Column Width

Set copy in columns approximately 40 characters wide. This matches natural reading patterns.

### Never Use Reverse Type

White text on black background reduces readership by up to 50%. It strains the eye.

### Serif Fonts for Body Copy

Ogilvy insisted on serif fonts (like Caslon or Baskerville) for body copy. He believed they were easier to read than sans-serif fonts.

### Never Set Body Copy in ALL CAPS

It is difficult to read. Use upper and lower case.

---

## The Body Copy Checklist

Before finalizing body copy, verify:

- [ ] Does it read like a personal letter?
- [ ] Does every paragraph earn its place?
- [ ] Are claims backed with facts ("Reason Why")?
- [ ] Is jargon eliminated?
- [ ] Are subheads used to break up text?
- [ ] Do all photos have captions?
- [ ] Is the language simple and direct?
- [ ] Would the reader learn something new?
