# Ogilvy's Big Idea Framework

> "Unless your campaign is built around a Big Idea, it will pass like a ship in the night." — David Ogilvy

## What Is a Big Idea?

A Big Idea is NOT:
- A visual pun
- A clever joke
- A tagline

A Big Idea IS:
- A durable, potent concept that can anchor a brand for decades
- A conceptual hook connecting the product to the consumer's subconscious desires
- A unique way of framing the product that competitors cannot copy

---

## The Big Idea Checklist

Ogilvy provided a rigorous test to determine if a concept qualifies as a Big Idea:

### 1. Did it make me gasp when I first saw it?

The visceral reaction is the first test of potency. If your first response is "that's interesting," it's not big enough.

### 2. Do I wish I had thought of it myself?

Professional envy is a strong indicator of quality. The best ideas make other professionals jealous.

### 3. Is it unique?

Does it carve out a distinct space in the market? If competitors could claim the same thing, it's not a Big Idea.

### 4. Does it fit the strategy to perfection?

Creativity without strategy is merely decoration. The Big Idea must serve the business objective.

### 5. Could it be used for 30 years?

This is the ultimate litmus test. Ogilvy loathed "short-term" thinking.

**Examples of Big Ideas that lasted:**
- The "Man in the Hathaway Shirt" (ran for decades)
- Dove's "1/4 Cleansing Cream" (still used today)
- Commander Whitehead for Schweppes (years of consistent use)

## Big Ideas in Headlines

The Big Idea often manifests in the headline as:

### A Unique Angle

Not "our car is quiet" but "At 60 miles an hour, the loudest noise comes from the electric clock."

### A Story Hook

Not "buy this shirt" but "The man in the Hathaway shirt" with the mysterious eyepatch.

### A Reframe

Not "soap" but "beauty bar with 1/4 cleansing cream."

## The Brand Image Connection

Every advertisement should contribute to the long-term personality of the brand.

> "Every advertisement should be thought of as a contribution to the complex symbol which is the brand image."

### The Implication

- Don't vacillate between different messages
- Each piece of communication should reinforce the same brand personality
- Short-term tactics that contradict the brand image are destructive

### Examples of Strong Brand Images

| Brand | Brand Image | How Maintained |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| Rolls-Royce | Engineering excellence, understated luxury | Every ad focused on specific engineering facts |
| Hathaway | Sophisticated, mysterious gentleman | Consistent use of aristocratic model with eyepatch |
| Schweppes | British sophistication | Commander Whitehead as consistent spokesperson |

## Finding Your Big Idea

### Step 1: Immerse in Research

Ogilvy spent three weeks reading technical reports before writing the Rolls-Royce headline. The "electric clock" fact came from an engineering review.

### Step 2: Look for the Unexpected Truth

The Big Idea is often hiding in plain sight—a fact so good it seems too simple:
- The car is so quiet the clock is the loudest thing
- The soap contains actual moisturizer
- The shirt is worn by aristocrats

### Step 3: Test the Longevity

Ask: "Can this idea sustain a campaign for years?"

If it's dependent on a trend, a current event, or a one-time joke, it's not a Big Idea.

## The Unique Selling Proposition (USP)

Related to the Big Idea is the USP—the single most compelling benefit.

### Characteristics of a Strong USP

1. **It must be unique** - Something competitors don't offer or haven't claimed
2. **It must be a proposition** - A specific promise to the consumer
3. **It must sell** - Strong enough to move people to action

### USP Examples

| Product | USP |
|---------|-----|
| Dove | "Contains 1/4 cleansing cream" |
| M&Ms | "Melts in your mouth, not in your hands" |
| FedEx | "When it absolutely, positively has to be there overnight" |

---

## Big Idea vs. Gimmick

| Big Idea | Gimmick |
|----------|---------|
| Lasts for years | One-time use |
| Reinforces brand | Distracts from brand |
| Based on product truth | Based on unrelated cleverness |
| Makes competitors jealous | Makes competitors relieved |
| Consumer remembers the product | Consumer remembers the ad but not the product |
