# Punchy Headlines & Subject Lines

> "On the average, five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy. When you have written your headline, you have spent eighty cents out of your dollar." — **David Ogilvy**

This skill blends **David Ogilvy's** research-backed advertising principles with **Emma Stratton's** "Make It Punchy" methodology. It stops the scroll and earns the click by treating the headline as the most valuable asset of your writing.

## When to Activate

Use this skill when asked to:
- Write email subject lines (cold emails, follow-ups, newsletters, re-engagement)
- Create article or blog post headlines
- Generate hooks for LinkedIn posts or social content
- Improve open rates or click-through rates
- Review/edit existing headlines for punch

- **punchy-copy-writer** skill: For full website copy and body content (use after headlines are set)
- **email-writer** skill: For complete email drafting (this skill feeds into it for subject lines)

## The Core Philosophy

### 1. The "80 Cents" Rule (Ogilvy)

If your headline fails, your content is invisible. Ogilvy often wrote 20+ headlines for a single ad. Never settle for the first draft.

**Implication:** Spend 80% of your effort on the headline. Generate at least 5-10 options before selecting the best.

### 2. The "Flag" Technique (Ogilvy)

Don't write for everyone. Flag down your specific reader. Ogilvy said: "Use the headline to flag the specific prospect."

| Too Broad | Flagged |
|-----------|---------|
| "How to manage finances." | "Advice for **Startups** dealing with **Cash Burn**." |
| "Tips for better meetings." | "For **Engineering Managers** who hate status meetings." |
| "Improve your health." | "To **Parents Over 40** who want more energy." |

### 3. The BBQ Test (Stratton)

Would you actually say this subject line to a friend at a BBQ?

| Fail | Pass |
|------|------|
| "Maximizing Synergies for Q3." | "How to get your team to work faster this quarter." |
| "Operational Excellence Framework" | "Stop wasting time on meetings that go nowhere." |
| "Leveraging AI for Growth" | "How AI can help you close deals faster." |

## The Four Functions of a Headline (Ogilvy)

Every headline must perform at least one of these functions:

1. **Select the Prospect:** Flag the specific reader (mothers, CTOs, homeowners)
2. **Promise a Benefit:** Answer "What's in it for me?"
3. **Deliver News:** Use words like New, Announcing, Finally, Now
4. **Offer Service/Information:** Teach something valuable ("How to...")

The best headlines do multiple functions at once.

## 5 High-Converting Headline Formulas

Use these as starting points, then refine using the BBQ test.

### Formula 1: The "How To" (The Utility Play)

**Why it works:** Pure utility. Ogilvy found "How to" headlines among the highest performing because they promise a specific solution.

**Structure:** How to [Benefit] without [Pain].

| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| Ogilvy Style | "How to Create Advertising That Sells" |
| Punchy Style | "How to get your nights and weekends back" |
| B2B Example | "How to close enterprise deals without discounting" |

### Formula 2: The "News" Hook

**Why it works:** Consumers are always looking for what is new. Use power words like *New, Announcing, Finally, Now, At Last*.

**Structure:** [News Word]: [The Big Change/Benefit].

| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| Ogilvy Style | "Announcing: The new Rolls-Royce..." |
| Punchy Style | "Finally: a way to track expenses that isn't boring." |
| B2B Example | "Now: One dashboard for your entire security stack." |

### Formula 3: The "Flag & Benefit" (The Dog Whistle)

**Why it works:** It calls out the specific "A+ Customer" and ignores everyone else.

**Structure:** [Audience Call Out]: [Big Promise].

| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| Ogilvy Style | "To men who want to quit work someday." |
| Punchy Style | "For Engineering Managers who hate status meetings." |
| B2B Example | "To CTOs tired of managing legacy infrastructure." |

### Formula 4: The Curiosity Gap (The Open Loop)

**Why it works:** It creates an itch the reader must scratch. *Caution:* Do not use if the content doesn't deliver.

**Structure:** The [Number/Adjective] secret to [Desire].

| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| Ogilvy Style | "The man in the Hathaway shirt." (Visual curiosity) |
| Punchy Style | "The one thing killing your team's velocity (it's not code quality)." |
| B2B Example | "The 3-minute trick our top performers use every morning." |

### Formula 5: The "Command" (The Action)

**Why it works:** Direct and authoritative. Tells the user exactly what to do.

**Structure:** Stop [Pain]. Start [Benefit].

| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| Ogilvy Style | "Give her a Arpege." |
| Punchy Style | "Stop drowning in tickets. Start automating support." |
| B2B Example | "Stop guessing. Start knowing where every deal stands." |

## Email Subject Line Specifics

Email subject lines face harder constraints than print headlines.

### 1. The Mobile Cutoff (40-60 characters)

Most people check email on phones. If your "punch" is at the end, it gets cut off.

| Bad (punch lost) | Good (front-loaded) |
|------------------|---------------------|
| "We are engaging in a new partnership that will help you save money." | "Save money: Our new partnership." |
| "Following up on our conversation from last week about the proposal" | "Re: the proposal - quick question" |
| "I wanted to share some exciting news about our platform updates" | "New: 3 features you asked for" |

### 2. The Preview Text (The "Subhead")

Ogilvy loved long headlines. In email, use the **Subject Line** as the hook and the **Preview Text** as the explanation.

| Subject Line | Preview Text |
|--------------|--------------|
| 5 ways to fix your SEO | Number 3 is the mistake most SaaS founders make... |
| Quick question about [Company] | Not selling anything, just curious about your approach to... |
| re: your LinkedIn post | The one about X - had a follow-up thought... |

### 3. Personalization That Works

| Generic | Personalized |
|---------|--------------|
| "Improve your sales process" | "[Name], noticed your team just expanded" |
| "New features available" | "For [Company]: the integration you requested" |
| "Let's connect" | "[Mutual connection] suggested we talk" |

## The "Ogilvy x Punchy" Checklist

Run every headline through this gauntlet before publishing:

### Ogilvy's Checks (The Science)

- [ ] **Is it specific?** ("Save $500" vs "Save Money")
- [ ] **Does it promise a benefit?** (Self-interest)
- [ ] **Is it "news"?** (Does it feel fresh?)
- [ ] **Does it "flag" the right reader?** (Dog whistle)
- [ ] **Is it free of puns?** (Ogilvy hated "tricky" headlines; clarity sells)
- [ ] **Does it include a number or specificity?** (10+ words often outperform short headlines)

### Stratton's Checks (The Soul)

- [ ] **The BBQ Test:** Does it sound like a human?
- [ ] **No Jargon:** Are words like "synergy," "paradigm," or "leverage" removed?
- [ ] **The "So What?"** Did you drill down to the real value?
- [ ] **Under 60 characters?** (For email subject lines)
- [ ] **Front-loaded punch?** (Key benefit appears early)

## Power Words: Ogilvy's Lexicon

Sprinkle (but don't stuff) these into your headlines.

| Category | Words |
|----------|-------|
| **News** | New, Now, Announcing, Introducing, Finally, At Last, Suddenly |
| **Utility** | How to, Why, Which, This, Advice, Facts |
| **Urgency** | Hurry, Last Chance, Only, Today, Limited |
| **Benefit** | Save, Easy, Quick, Guaranteed, Proven, Results, Free |
| **Emotion** | Darling, Love, Fear, Proud, Wanted, Secret |
| **Logic** | Because, Consequently, Therefore, Due to |

### Words to Avoid (Jargon/Buzzwords)

| Instead of... | Try... |
|---------------|--------|
| Leverage | Use |
| Synergy | Working together |
| Optimize | Improve, Boost |
| Transform | Change, Fix |
| Revolutionize | A better way to... |
| Enable | Help, Let |
| Utilize | Use |
| Facilitate | Make easier |
| End-to-end | Complete, Full |

## Output Format

When generating headlines, deliver:

### Standard Output

```
CONTEXT: [Brief description of what the headline is for]
AUDIENCE: [Who this is flagging]

HEADLINE OPTIONS (ranked by recommendation):

1. [Best option] — [Why this works]
2. [Second option] — [Why this works]  
3. [Third option] — [Why this works]
4. [Fourth option] — [Alternative angle]
5. [Fifth option] — [Alternative angle]

RECOMMENDED: #[Number] because [brief rationale]
```

### For Email Subject Lines, Also Include:

```
SUBJECT LINE: [The headline]
PREVIEW TEXT: [The supporting text that appears after]
CHARACTER COUNT: [Number]
```

### Batch Generation (When Asked for Multiple)

Generate 10 options using varied formulas:
- 2-3 "How To" variations
- 2 "News" hooks
- 2 "Flag & Benefit" options
- 2 "Curiosity Gap" options
- 1-2 "Command" options

## Process: How to Use This Skill

### Step 1: Understand the Context

Before writing, clarify:
- What is being promoted/communicated?
- Who is the A+ reader? (Be specific)
- What is the one action you want them to take?
- What is their urgent pain or desire?

### Step 2: Generate Options

Never settle for the first headline. Generate at least 5-10 variations using different formulas.

### Step 3: Apply the Checklist

Run top candidates through the Ogilvy x Punchy checklist.

### Step 4: Test Specificity

Ask: "Can I make this MORE specific?" Replace vague benefits with concrete numbers, outcomes, or timeframes.

### Step 5: Read Aloud

Does it sound like something a human would say? If it sounds like corporate-speak, rewrite.
